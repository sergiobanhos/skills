# Viral Product Strategist

Use this skill when the user wants to create, improve, audit, reposition, launch, or rewrite a product, landing page, SaaS, digital product, app, CLI, course, template, community, marketplace, or marketing asset using viral-product principles.

The skill is based on the idea that viral products are not merely “good products”. They are products that are easy to understand, easy to remember, easy to talk about, easy to buy, and easy to share.

The 32 principles are patterns, not laws. Use them as a compass. Apply judgment based on product type, market, audience, price point, proof available, and business model.

## Core job

Help the user turn a product into something people can understand in seconds, remember the next day, want to try, trust enough to buy, and feel compelled to share.

The skill can produce:

- Viral product audit
- Landing page rewrite
- Hero section rewrite
- Pricing strategy
- Product positioning
- Paywall strategy
- Demo/try-before-buy strategy
- OG image concept
- Footer/share ending
- Launch checklist
- Viral product scorecard
- Copy variants
- Competitor comparison table
- Testimonial collection plan
- Product naming critique

## When to invoke

Invoke this skill when the user asks for help with any of the following:

- “Make this product more viral”
- “Audit my landing page”
- “Improve my SaaS positioning”
- “Rewrite my hero section”
- “Create a landing page for this product”
- “Help me price this product”
- “Make this product easier to sell”
- “Create copy for launch”
- “Make my offer stronger”
- “Analyze this product idea”
- “Improve conversion”
- “Create a SKILL/playbook for viral products”
- “Apply the 32 principles of viral products”

Do not invoke it for unrelated product work such as backend architecture, legal compliance, financial modeling, or technical implementation unless the user explicitly wants the viral-product/marketing angle.

## Required inputs

Ask for missing inputs only when they materially change the answer. If the user gives partial information, proceed with clearly labeled assumptions.

Ideal inputs:

1. Product name
2. Product type: SaaS, app, course, template, CLI, marketplace, service, plugin, physical product, etc.
3. Target customer
4. Pain/desire the product solves
5. Current landing page copy or URL, if available
6. Pricing model and price
7. Main competitors or alternatives
8. Current proof: testimonials, revenue, users, case studies, waitlist, demos, screenshots
9. Main conversion goal: buy, analyze, generate, book call, join waitlist, install, upload file, etc.
10. Distribution channel: X/Twitter, LinkedIn, Instagram, SEO, Product Hunt, ads, communities, influencers, founder audience, cold outbound, etc.

Minimum viable input:

- What the product does
- Who it is for
- What the user wants improved

If even the minimum is missing, ask for a compact clarification:

“Me manda em 1 frase: o que o produto faz, pra quem é, e qual ação você quer que a pessoa tome.”

## Operating principles

Follow these rules while using the skill:

1. Be direct, concrete, and opinionated.
2. Prefer clear claims over vague praise.
3. Replace adjectives with numbers whenever possible.
4. If numbers are unknown, use placeholders or suggest what to measure. Do not invent metrics.
5. Treat the hero section as the highest-leverage surface.
6. Treat the product demo as more persuasive than explanation.
7. Reduce choices. One product promise. One main CTA. Three pricing options max.
8. Make the product easy to describe in under 10 words.
9. Make the copy sound like the founder or customer, not a corporate template.
10. Use the principles as patterns, not rigid rules.
11. When a principle conflicts with business reality, explain the tradeoff.
12. Optimize for memorability, not just clarity.
13. Optimize for purchase behavior, not just signups.
14. Optimize for shareability, not just conversion.
15. Sell a human desire: money, time, health, status, relief, certainty, belonging, speed, simplicity, beauty, control, or less pain.

## The 32 principles, operationalized

Use this section as the core knowledge base. Each principle includes what it means, how to apply it, what to avoid, and examples.

### 1. A viral product does not have a free plan

Meaning:
Free plans can inflate vanity metrics while increasing support, infrastructure cost, and feature pressure from people who are not paying. The principle argues that validation comes from payment, not signups.

How to apply:
- Prefer paid trials, one-time payments, credits, usage packs, or a low-ticket entry offer.
- Remove unlimited free usage unless it is a deliberate acquisition channel with proven conversion economics.
- Make the user pay before consuming expensive resources.
- If you need free access, restrict it to a demo, sample, calculator, preview, or sandbox.

Avoid:
- “Free forever” plans for heavy users.
- Supporting users who will never buy.
- Hiding behind “we have users” when nobody pays.

Examples:
- Weak: “Create unlimited AI images for free.”
- Strong: “Generate 3 previews free. Pay R$19 to export the final pack.”
- Strong: “Analyze your landing page for free. Unlock the full rewrite for R$49.”

Tradeoff:
A free plan can work when the product has strong network effects, near-zero marginal cost, or a clear upgrade path. If those are absent, default to no free plan.

### 2. A viral product has three colors

Meaning:
Every extra color competes for attention. A viral page uses visual restraint so the CTA and product value stand out.

How to apply:
- Use black/dark text, white/light background, and one accent color for the CTA or key highlight.
- Use neutral grays for structure, borders, and secondary text.
- Make the buy/action button visually obvious.

Avoid:
- Rainbow gradients everywhere.
- Multiple button colors.
- Decorative color that distracts from conversion.

Example palette:
- Text: near black
- Background: white/off-white
- Accent: electric blue, green, orange, purple, or brand color

Example:
- Weak: hero with red button, blue links, green icons, purple cards, yellow badges.
- Strong: black text, white background, one blue “Analyze My Website” button.

### 3. A viral product uses numbers instead of adjectives

Meaning:
Adjectives are vague. Numbers create specificity, memory, and credibility.

How to apply:
- Replace “fast” with “in 42 seconds”.
- Replace “save time” with “save 4 hours/week”.
- Replace “better conversion” with “turn 1 landing page into 12 testable variants”.
- Replace “affordable” with “R$49 one-time”.

Avoid:
- Inventing numbers.
- Using fake precision.
- Saying “best”, “fast”, “easy”, “powerful” without proof.

Examples:
- Weak: “Create professional product photos quickly.”
- Strong: “Turn 1 phone photo into 12 store-ready product shots in 2 minutes.”
- Weak: “Improve your CV.”
- Strong: “Find the 27 keywords missing from your CV before you apply.”

### 4. A viral product ends with a footer people want to share

Meaning:
Most visitors will not buy, but they may remember or share the final impression. The footer is the last emotional beat.

How to apply:
- End with a strong line, memeable statement, bold belief, or memorable founder note.
- Make the final CTA specific.
- Add a small “share this with...” prompt when appropriate.

Avoid:
- Ending with generic links only.
- Footer copy like “© 2026 All rights reserved” as the last impression.

Examples:
- “Your landing page should sell before your sales call starts.”
- “Stop shipping features nobody can explain.”
- “Send this to the founder still using ‘Get Started’ as a CTA.”

Footer structure:
```markdown
Bold belief line.
One-sentence product reminder.
Final CTA.
Tiny share prompt.
```

### 5. A viral product treats the OG image as a YouTube thumbnail

Meaning:
The OG image is often seen before the site. It must earn the click in feeds, DMs, Slack, WhatsApp, X, LinkedIn, and Discord.

How to apply:
- Design the OG image as a high-contrast thumbnail.
- Use a big visual hook, short text, clear before/after, human face, surprising result, or strong number.
- Make it understandable at small sizes.
- Use 1 idea, not a full website screenshot.

Avoid:
- Tiny text.
- Generic logos on gradients.
- Screenshots nobody can read.

Examples:
- “Your CV is missing 31 keywords” with a red-marked CV preview.
- “This photo became a catalog shoot” with before/after product image.
- “Your landing page loses 80% here” with a hero section teardown.

OG image checklist:
- Can someone understand it in 2 seconds?
- Would it make someone click from a WhatsApp preview?
- Is the text readable on mobile?
- Does it show the product outcome?

### 6. A viral product reveals one idea per screen

Meaning:
Each viewport should communicate a single idea. Cognitive overload kills curiosity.

How to apply:
- Make each section answer one question.
- Use one headline per section.
- Avoid stuffing features, testimonials, pricing, and FAQ into the same screen.
- Think like an Instagram carousel: one slide, one message.

Suggested sequence:
1. Hero: what it is and why I want it.
2. Problem: you understand my pain.
3. Demo: I can see it working.
4. Outcome: what I get.
5. Proof: people like me trust it.
6. Pricing: how I buy.
7. Comparison: why this over alternatives.
8. Footer: why I remember/share it.

### 7. A viral product has a headline a fifth grader can understand

Meaning:
Simple language travels further. If a non-expert cannot understand the headline, it will not spread.

How to apply:
- Use familiar words.
- Avoid jargon, acronyms, and clever phrasing that requires explanation.
- Say what changes in the customer’s life.

Avoid:
- “AI-powered productivity orchestration platform for workflow acceleration.”
- “Next-gen multimodal enablement layer for enterprise teams.”

Examples:
- Weak: “AI-driven applicant tracking optimization suite.”
- Strong: “Make your CV pass the robot before a recruiter sees it.”
- Weak: “Autonomous dev workflow interface.”
- Strong: “Tell the AI what to build. It edits your code for you.”

Fifth-grader test:
Ask: “Could a smart 10-year-old explain this to someone else?”

### 8. A viral product has a hard paywall

Meaning:
Payment is validation. Signups without payment can create false confidence.

How to apply:
- Ask for payment before collecting long onboarding data when the value is obvious.
- Put the purchase moment early.
- Use preview/demo to create desire, then charge to unlock the complete result.

Avoid:
- Long signup forms before value.
- Letting users consume the full result before paying.
- Measuring validation by waitlist only.

Examples:
- Strong: “Upload your CV → see score preview → pay R$14,90 to download optimized CV.”
- Strong: “Paste your landing page → get 3 obvious leaks free → pay to receive full rewrite.”

### 9. A viral product has copy only you could write

Meaning:
Generic copy is invisible. Founder-specific experience, customer language, and unique beliefs make copy harder to clone.

How to apply:
- Include the founder’s lived insight.
- Name the exact annoying problem.
- Use specific stories, mistakes, constraints, and opinions.
- Write lines competitors would be afraid or unable to write.

Avoid:
- “We help businesses grow with AI.”
- “Powerful, easy-to-use platform.”
- “Streamline your workflow.”

Examples:
- Generic: “Create better software with AI.”
- Founder-specific: “Built after watching devs burn R$500 in tokens just to rename a button.”
- Generic: “Improve your job applications.”
- Founder-specific: “Most CVs don’t fail because you’re bad. They fail because the robot never found the words it was looking for.”

### 10. A viral product shows the product before it explains it

Meaning:
A demo communicates faster than paragraphs. Let the user see the thing working.

How to apply:
- Put a screenshot, video, GIF, interactive demo, generated output, before/after, or sample result above the fold or immediately after the hero.
- Show the input and output.
- Let users try a lightweight version on the landing page.

Avoid:
- Abstract illustrations instead of product visuals.
- Feature lists before demo.
- Explaining the dashboard without showing it.

Examples:
- Before/after CV analysis.
- Phone photo → professional catalog image.
- Prompt → generated code diff.
- Raw invoice PDF → extracted JSON.

### 11. A viral product does one thing

Meaning:
People remember focused products. If you solve too many problems at once, users cannot describe you.

How to apply:
- Choose the single most memorable job.
- Make every section support that job.
- Hide or defer secondary features.

Avoid:
- “CRM + AI chatbot + analytics + payments + automations + marketplace.”
- Hero sections with 5 different promises.

Examples:
- Weak: “The complete AI platform for your business.”
- Strong: “Turn WhatsApp conversations into paid orders.”
- Weak: “All-in-one creator monetization platform.”
- Strong: “Sell your first digital product from one link.”

### 12. A viral product uses Popcorn Pricing

Meaning:
Pricing should feel as easy as buying popcorn: Good, Better, Best. Too many choices slow the purchase.

How to apply:
- Use three tiers max.
- Make the middle option the obvious default.
- Differentiate tiers by outcome, volume, speed, support, or status.
- Avoid forcing visitors to study a spreadsheet.

Structure:
```markdown
Good — for trying it once
Better — best value / most popular
Best — for power users or teams
```

Examples:
- CV product:
  - 1 CV: R$14,90
  - 3 CVs: R$29,90
  - 10 CVs: R$79,90
- Landing page audit:
  - Roast: R$49
  - Rewrite: R$149
  - Full launch kit: R$399

Avoid:
- 6 tiers.
- Feature matrices with 40 rows.
- Hidden pricing.

### 13. A viral product rides a wave

Meaning:
It is easier to sell something people are already discussing. Trends do part of the marketing.

How to apply:
- Attach the product to a visible shift: AI agents, creator economy, job market, WhatsApp commerce, compliance changes, remote work, cost reduction, no-code, privacy, etc.
- Use the wave in the headline, demo, and launch story.
- Do not chase trends unrelated to the customer’s pain.

Examples:
- “AI coding agents are powerful, but token costs are exploding. This CLI helps teams code with AI without burning their margin.”
- “Every company wants AI support. Most WhatsApp stores still answer customers manually.”

Wave test:
Can you name the conversation already happening without your product?

### 14. A viral product steals its best copy from customers

Meaning:
Customers describe pain in clearer, more emotional words than marketers do.

How to apply:
- Mine calls, emails, DMs, support tickets, reviews, comments, Reddit, X, WhatsApp, and sales conversations.
- Use exact phrases when possible.
- Turn customer complaints into section headlines.

Avoid:
- Corporate language when customers use blunt language.
- Assuming your words are better than the customer’s.

Customer-copy mining template:
```markdown
Customer phrase:
Pain behind it:
Emotion:
Possible headline:
Possible CTA:
Possible FAQ:
```

Examples:
- Customer says: “I apply to 50 jobs and nobody even opens my CV.”
- Headline: “Stop sending CVs that never reach a human.”

### 15. A viral product has a founder people can see and hear

Meaning:
People trust people. A founder screen recording can outperform polished corporate videos.

How to apply:
- Add a founder demo video.
- Use founder face in launch content and possibly OG image.
- Explain the product like a founder talking to another human, not a brand talking to a market.

Video script structure:
```markdown
1. “I built this because...”
2. Show the painful old way.
3. Show the product doing the job.
4. Show the result.
5. Say exactly who it is for.
6. CTA: “Try [specific action].”
```

Examples:
- “I got tired of seeing great dev tools die because nobody understood the landing page. So I built this.”
- “Here’s me turning a bad phone photo into a catalog image in 90 seconds.”

### 16. A viral product makes pricing impossible to miss

Meaning:
Visitors look for pricing to understand what kind of product this is. Hidden pricing creates uncertainty.

How to apply:
- Put “Pricing” in the header.
- Put the starting price or pricing section early enough.
- Make the price visually obvious.
- Use pricing to explain value.

Avoid:
- “Contact us” for simple products.
- Pricing buried after long scroll.
- Hiding price because you are afraid of objection.

Examples:
- Header: Product | Examples | Pricing | Testimonials
- CTA: “Analyze my CV — R$14,90”

### 17. A viral product has a headline people remember the next day

Meaning:
The best headline sticks. It should be easy to recall and repeat.

How to apply:
- Write at least 5 headline options.
- Show them to people.
- Ask 24 hours later which one they remember.
- Keep the one with recall, not necessarily the cleverest one.

Headline formats:
- “Stop [painful behavior].”
- “Turn [bad input] into [desired output].”
- “The [product category] for people who [specific desire].”
- “[Number] [thing] in [time] without [pain].”
- “Your [asset] is losing [outcome] because [surprising reason].”

Examples:
- “Stop sending CVs that robots reject.”
- “Turn one ugly product photo into a full catalog shoot.”
- “The AI coding CLI for devs who burn too many tokens.”

### 18. A viral product has an emotional headline

Meaning:
People remember feelings: surprise, anger, relief, laughter, status, fear, ambition, curiosity.

How to apply:
- Make the headline provoke: “wow”, “finally”, “that’s me”, “what?”, “ouch”, or laughter.
- Use emotional truth, not manipulation.

Avoid:
- Sterile benefit statements.
- Feature-first headlines.

Examples:
- Safe: “Create better product photos with AI.”
- Emotional: “Your product photos look cheaper than your product.”
- Safe: “AI landing page analyzer.”
- Emotional: “Your hero section is probably killing your launch.”

### 19. A viral product does something people have never seen before

Meaning:
People do not share ordinary clones. Surprise creates conversation.

How to apply:
- Add a “magic moment” that feels new.
- Visualize transformation instantly.
- Create an output that is inherently shareable.
- Make the demo look like something a viewer wants to show someone else.

Avoid:
- Copying the category leader with a different color.
- Selling “same thing but cheaper”.

Examples:
- Upload a messy WhatsApp chat → product creates a checkout-ready order summary.
- Paste a job description → product highlights exactly why your CV will be rejected.
- Record founder talking → product generates launch page, OG image concept, and pricing table.

Magic moment test:
Would someone watching the demo say: “Wait, how did it do that?”

### 20. A viral product can be sold from the hero section alone

Meaning:
Most visitors will not scroll. The hero must explain the product, desire, proof, and next action within seconds.

How to apply:
Hero must answer:
1. What is it?
2. Who is it for?
3. What do I get?
4. Why should I trust it?
5. What happens when I click?

Hero formula:
```markdown
Headline: Clear + memorable + outcome-driven.
Subheadline: Who it is for + how it works + concrete result.
CTA: Specific next action.
Visual: Product/demo/outcome.
Proof: Testimonial, number, usage, founder credibility, or example.
```

Example:
```markdown
Headline: Stop sending CVs that robots reject.
Subheadline: Paste a job description and get a CV optimized with the missing keywords recruiters actually search for.
CTA: Analyze My CV
Visual: Before/after ATS score and highlighted keywords.
Proof: Used by 50,000+ candidates.
```

### 21. A viral product shows empathy before it sells

Meaning:
People trust your solution after they believe you understand their pain.

How to apply:
- Describe the problem in the customer’s language.
- Name the exact frustration.
- Show the old painful way before showing the product.

Avoid:
- Jumping straight into “our solution”.
- Describing generic problems everyone claims to solve.

Examples:
- “You don’t know if your CV is bad, ignored, or just invisible to the ATS.”
- “You spent hours making the product. Then your photo makes it look like a dropshipping scam.”

Empathy formula:
```markdown
You’re trying to [desired outcome].
But every time you [current behavior], [specific painful thing happens].
The worst part: [hidden frustration].
```

### 22. A viral product has one call to action

Meaning:
Multiple CTAs create hesitation. One next step creates motion.

How to apply:
- Choose one primary action.
- Repeat it across the page.
- Use secondary links only when they do not compete with the main goal.

Avoid:
- “Get Started”, “Book a Demo”, “Learn More”, “Join Waitlist”, and “Contact Sales” all in the same hero.
- Equal visual weight for multiple buttons.

Examples:
- Weak: “Get Started” + “Learn More” + “Contact Us”
- Strong: “Analyze My Website”
- Strong: “Generate My Catalog Photos”
- Strong: “Buy the Template”

### 23. A viral product has a name people remember

Meaning:
Names spread when they are easy to say, spell, remember, and connect to the promise.

How to apply:
- Prefer real words or familiar combinations.
- Keep it short.
- Avoid names that require explanation.
- Check pronunciation, spelling, domain, and social handle.

Avoid:
- Overly clever wordplay.
- Random AI-sounding names.
- Names people misspell after hearing once.

Examples:
- Strong categories: “Cursor”, “Linear”, “Notion”, “Framer”, “Bolt”.
- Product-name patterns:
  - Outcome word: “Ship”, “Launch”, “Score”, “Proof”
  - Tool metaphor: “Compass”, “Radar”, “Lens”, “Forge”
  - Customer language: “CV por Vaga”, “Link in Bio”, “Catalog Maker”

Name test:
Can a user say “Use [name]” in a sentence without explaining it?

### 24. A viral product sells a human desire, not a feature

Meaning:
Features are vehicles. Desire is what people buy.

Core desires:
- More money
- More time
- Better health
- More status
- Less pain
- More certainty
- More control
- More beauty
- More belonging
- More speed
- Less embarrassment
- Less risk

How to apply:
- Translate every feature into a desire.
- Lead with the outcome.
- Make the user feel the before/after.

Examples:
- Feature: “ATS keyword analyzer.”
- Desire: “Stop being rejected before a human sees your CV.”
- Feature: “AI image generation.”
- Desire: “Make your store look expensive without paying for a photoshoot.”
- Feature: “AI coding CLI.”
- Desire: “Ship faster without burning your team’s token budget.”

### 25. A viral product lets people try the product before buying it

Meaning:
The landing page should contain the experience, not just describe it. Let people feel the product before paying.

How to apply:
- Add an interactive demo.
- Show a sample input and output.
- Let users generate a preview.
- Offer a calculator, analyzer, playground, or before/after.

Avoid:
- Hiding all value behind login.
- Asking for account creation before any proof.

Examples:
- Landing page grader: paste URL → get 3 free leaks → pay for full teardown.
- CV tool: paste job description → see missing keywords → pay for optimized CV.
- Product photo tool: upload photo → see watermarked preview → pay to export.

### 26. A viral product does not use weak words

Meaning:
Weak words blur the claim. Strong copy is concrete enough to picture, remember, and challenge.

Weak words:
- most
- many
- rarely
- often
- usually
- some
- various
- lots of
- easy
- fast
- powerful
- robust
- seamless
- innovative
- scalable
- cutting-edge
- optimized
- world-class

How to apply:
- Replace weak estimates with specific claims.
- Use either a number, a concrete behavior, or a vivid image.
- If proof is missing, make a precise qualitative claim without pretending certainty.

Examples:
- Weak: “Many founders struggle with landing pages.”
- Strong: “Your hero has 5 seconds to sell the product.”
- Weak: “Create content faster.”
- Strong: “Turn 1 product idea into 10 launch posts.”

### 27. A viral product does not have a subscription

Meaning:
Subscriptions create buying resistance because people already pay for many recurring products. One-time payments are easier to impulse-buy and share.

How to apply:
- Prefer one-time payments, credit packs, lifetime deals, templates, reports, audits, exports, or pay-per-result when possible.
- Use subscription only when ongoing value is obvious and necessary.

Avoid:
- Defaulting to monthly billing because SaaS does.
- Charging monthly for a one-time job.

Examples:
- Strong: “R$49 one-time landing page roast.”
- Strong: “R$14,90 per optimized CV.”
- Strong: “R$79 pack with 10 generations.”

Subscription is justified when:
- The product monitors continuously.
- The user gets recurring value.
- Infrastructure costs recur with usage.
- Team collaboration or ongoing workflow creates retention.

### 28. A viral product has a CTA that says what happens next

Meaning:
Generic CTAs create uncertainty. Specific CTAs reduce friction.

How to apply:
- Replace “Get Started” with the action the user is about to take.
- Use verbs tied to the product’s magic moment.

CTA formulas:
- Analyze my [asset]
- Generate my [output]
- Roast my [thing]
- Build my [thing]

### 30. A viral product can be described in under 10 words

Meaning:
If users cannot describe it simply, they will not spread it.

How to apply:
- Create a 10-word description.
- Use it in the header, OG image, launch post, and footer.
- Remove secondary features until the sentence is clean.

- Under 10 words.
- No jargon.
- Contains the outcome.
- A customer can repeat it.

How to apply:
- Compare against direct competitors and common alternatives.

- Dishonest comparisons.

- Comparing only against weak alternatives.

### 32. A viral product is more expensive than its competitors

Meaning:
Price signals positioning. Being the cheapest rarely creates conversation or trust. Premium products are easier to remember when the outcome is valuable.

How to apply:
- Charge based on desired outcome, not only cost.
- Use premium framing: faster, simpler, more specific, better result, founder-led, done-for-you, higher status.
- If charging more, make the reason obvious.

Avoid:
- Competing only on price.
- “Same as X, but cheaper.”
- Premium pricing with weak proof.

Examples:
- Weak: “We are cheaper than other AI tools.”
- Strong: “Costs more because it gives you the final asset, not another dashboard.”
- Strong: “R$149 because one better application can be worth a new job.”

Premium justification checklist:
- Is the pain expensive?
- Is the result tied to money/time/status/risk?
- Is proof visible?
- Is the product simpler than alternatives?
- Is the category crowded with cheap clones?

## Viral product audit scoring rubric

Use this when scoring products.

Score each item from 0 to 10.

### Clarity
0–3: Visitor cannot explain the product.
4–6: Product is understandable but generic or bloated.
7–8: Clear promise, audience, and action.
9–10: Understandable in seconds and repeatable in under 10 words.

### Memorability
0–3: Generic category copy.
4–6: Some clear wording but no sticky phrase.
7–8: Strong headline or number.
9–10: People can remember and repeat the core line tomorrow.

### Desire
0–3: Sells features.
4–6: Mentions benefits but not deep desire.
7–8: Clear tie to money, time, status, health, relief, or pain.
9–10: Creates emotional urgency.

### Demo strength
0–3: No demo or only abstract illustration.
4–6: Screenshots exist but are unclear.
7–8: Strong before/after or product visual.
9–10: Product is instantly understood by seeing it.

### Pricing friction
0–3: Hidden/confusing pricing or too many plans.
4–6: Pricing visible but too complex or weak.
7–8: Clear three-tier or one-time model.
9–10: Pricing reinforces the value and buying is obvious.

### Proof
0–3: No proof.
4–6: Generic testimonials or vague numbers.
7–8: Specific testimonials, metrics, examples, or founder credibility.
9–10: Proof makes purchase feel low-risk.

### Shareability
0–3: Nothing worth sharing.
4–6: Decent page but no share hooks.
7–8: Strong OG, footer, or demo moment.
9–10: The product has a built-in “send this to someone” quality.

### Differentiation
0–3: Clone or commodity.
4–6: Some niche angle.
7–8: Clear difference from alternatives.
9–10: Has a surprising or category-defining angle.

### CTA focus
0–3: Many competing CTAs.
4–6: Main CTA exists but generic.
7–8: One clear action repeated.
9–10: CTA describes the magic moment and removes uncertainty.

### Founder/customer voice
0–3: Corporate/generic.
4–6: Some natural language.
7–8: Feels specific to this founder/customer.
9–10: Copy could not be copy-pasted by a competitor.

6. Write a subheadline that explains who it is for, how it works, and what result it creates.

## Pricing strategy guide

Use this sequence:

1. Determine whether the product is one-time, usage-based, credit-based, or truly recurring.
2. Avoid subscription by default unless the value is recurring.
3. Remove free plan unless there is a strategic reason to keep it.
4. Use a paid preview, one-time purchase, or credits if the product has variable AI/server cost.
5. Use Popcorn Pricing when there are multiple purchase levels.
6. Make the middle option the obvious best value.
7. Make price visible in header or early section.
8. Charge more if the outcome is valuable and competitors are commoditized.

Pricing examples:

```markdown
Best: 10 results — R$99
```


### AI generation product
```markdown

Better: 100 credits — R$99 — Best value
Best: 500 credits — R$399
```

### Subscription only when justified
Use subscription when:
- The product monitors something continuously.
- The customer gets value every week/month.

- Infrastructure costs recur.
- Retention is tied to ongoing workflow.

## Common anti-patterns and fixes

### Anti-pattern: “All-in-one platform”
Problem: Nobody remembers what it does.
Fix: Pick the one painful job that creates the strongest desire.

### Anti-pattern: “Get Started” CTA
Problem: The user does not know what happens next.
Fix: Use an action CTA: “Analyze My Website”, “Generate My Photos”, “Buy the Template”.

### Anti-pattern: Hidden pricing
Problem: Visitors assume it is expensive, complex, or enterprise-only.
Fix: Put “Pricing” in the header and show the price clearly.

### Anti-pattern: Generic AI copy
Problem: Every competitor can copy it.
Fix: Use founder insight, customer phrases, and specific painful details.

### Anti-pattern: Too many colors
Problem: Nothing feels important.
Fix: Use black text, white background, one accent color.

### Anti-pattern: Free plan for expensive AI product
Problem: Costs rise before validation.
Fix: Preview free, export paid. Or sell credits.

### Anti-pattern: Feature-first page
Problem: Users do not care yet.
Fix: Lead with desire, pain, demo, and result.

### Anti-pattern: No testimonials
Problem: Cold visitors must trust blindly.
Fix: Get 3 beta testimonials before driving traffic.

### Anti-pattern: No comparison
Problem: Users cannot justify switching.
Fix: Compare against manual work, generic AI, agencies, or competitors.
